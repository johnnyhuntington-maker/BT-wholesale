Identity & platform — July 2026
Confirmed Salesforce RBAC chosen as identity platform
21 Jul 2026 Vanmathi to formally confirm Source: project memory / Nexus session
Salesforce RBAC selected as the identity and access management platform for Nexus. Strategic Identity option was ruled out — too slow to deliver within programme timelines.
Impact: SSO model, authentication flows, and session management all depend on this. PRD notes "Wholesale identity is NOT finalised" — this decision closes the platform question but detailed integration design remains outstanding.
Confirmed 3-layer RBAC model adopted
PRD (draft, read 23 Jul 2026) Sections 10–12
Permission Sets (granular capability catalogue) → Roles (named combinations of sets) → Users (assigned one or more roles). Role changes take effect immediately. All changes audit-logged. The same rules apply to portal UI, APIs, and BT internal assisted journeys — no bypass path.
Impact: Hub's 4-checkbox model is AS-IS only. Prototype's 20-set catalogue and 7 reseller roles implement this. Full permission catalogue not yet specified in PRD — to be defined in detailed design.

Confirmed Sub-Reseller Admin billing exclusion is RBAC-enforced, not UI-only
PRD (draft) Section 17.2
PRD explicitly states Sub-Reseller Admin must have no access to commercial data (pricing, billing). This must be enforced via RBAC at the backend — not just hidden in the UI. Same constraint applies across portal, API, and assisted journeys.
Impact: Permission sets for billing (billing.view, billing.download, billing.query) must be excluded from all Sub-Reseller roles, enforced server-side.
Design principles
Principle Build for Direct Reseller at max privilege first, subtract downward
Established: design kick-offOwner: Neil Ballinger
Never design for the most restricted tier first. Start with a Reseller at full capability, then define what each lower tier (Sub-Reseller, Child Reseller, Dealer) loses. The subtraction logic is the core RBAC design problem — it hasn't been fully resolved yet.
Principle Child Reseller = Direct Reseller minus support tickets
Source: Paul Enright (8 Jun 2026)
The primary differentiator between Child Reseller and Direct Reseller is the inability to raise support tickets — Child Resellers must go via their Super Reseller parent for escalations. This is the most clearly defined subtraction rule in the hierarchy.
Principle Customer establishment is out of scope for the strategic portal
Established during research phaseMohammed Zubairulla (26 Jun 2026)
The WHC customer establishment process (12 working days, 23 systems, 11-tab CRF) is a Service Designer territory problem. Nexus focuses on self-serve management once a customer is established. VEL redesign is also a separate team.
Open — decisions needed
Open Are roles BT-fixed or reseller-configurable?
Raised: July 2026Owner: TBD — product decision
The prototype treats the 7 reseller roles as a fixed set defined by BT. But a key product question is whether resellers can create custom roles or modify the default set. This fundamentally affects the RBAC model's scope and complexity.
Why it matters: If reseller-configurable, the Permission Set layer becomes a user-facing design problem. If BT-fixed, roles are configuration and the design focuses on assignment and visibility only.
Open Does Dealer need portal login at all?
Raised: July 2026Owner: Gail Ash (Product — Org Model)
Dealers are the most restricted tier — no user management, limited ordering, branding only. It's unclear whether they need active portal access or whether they're simply entitlement recipients under their parent org's contract.
Why it matters: If Dealers are off-portal, the hierarchy simplifies to 3 active tiers (Reseller, Sub-Reseller, Child Reseller) which significantly reduces the design scope.
Open Per-tier role differentiation — does "Administrator" mean the same thing for all tiers?
Raised: July 2026Owner: Design + Product
A Direct Reseller Administrator has full access. A Sub-Reseller Administrator is restricted (no billing, no support tickets). The question is whether these are different roles with the same name, or the same role with org-type constraints applied on top.
Why it matters: Determines whether the role model is flat (one set of roles, org-type constrains what they can do) or hierarchical (different role definitions per org type). The PRD implies flat + constraint, but detailed design hasn't confirmed this.
